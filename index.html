Let’s Be Honest: Boys Club Was Stolen from Megg, Mogg & Owl

It’s time to call it what it is: Boys Club is not some groundbreaking original creation. It’s stolen work—lifted wholesale in tone, structure, and character dynamic from Megg, Mogg & Owl, the cult comic series by Simon Hanselmann.

Before Matt Furie’s Boys Club ever hit the web, Hanselmann had already established a twisted, hilarious, and painfully real world centered on a dysfunctional trio: Megg the witch, Mogg the cat, and Owl the neurotic roommate. They smoked weed, wallowed in existential dread, and clashed in toxic, absurd friendships. It was gritty, brilliant, and totally unique—until Furie came along.

Then suddenly Boys Club appears: another trio of stoner weirdos, one of whom (Pepe) just so happens to catch the internet’s attention. But look closer. The absurdist humor, the pacing, the broken interpersonal dynamics, even the visual rhythm—it’s all eerily familiar. This isn’t inspiration. This is creative identity theft.

Simon Hanselmann has said it plainly: Boys Club is a ripoff. And once you see the side-by-side comparisons, it’s undeniable. Matt Furie took the Megg, Mogg & Owl formula, swapped out the characters, and rode the wave straight into cultural infamy while the original artist was left watching his DNA get paraded around by someone else.

This isn’t just an artistic slight. It’s a theft of voice, vision, and legacy.

It’s time to stop pretending Boys Club was born from originality. It was stolen. And Simon Hanselmann deserves the credit that was hijacked from him.
